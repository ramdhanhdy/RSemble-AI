Why Rust's Borrow Checker Pays Off

The borrow checker is often experienced as a wall early on, but its real value shows up months later: it converts an entire class of bugs — use-after-free, double-free, data races — into compile-time errors. That trade is the whole argument.

Three threads emerged consistently across the candidate drafts. First, ownership makes resource lifetimes explicit in the type system rather than in comments. Second, refactoring becomes safer because the compiler proves the change is sound. Third, long-term maintenance cost drops sharply once the codebase compiles.

A useful analogy surfaced from one model: think of the checker as a strict librarian — annoying at checkout, indispensable when the shelves stay organized.
